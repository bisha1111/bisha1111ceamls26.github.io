what_i_learned: |
  Today, we focused on implementing EfficientNetV2, comparing the deep learning models we have trained, and preparing for our upcoming DermaBridge Weekly Showcase. In the morning, we reviewed the EfficientNetV2 architecture and began setting it up with our skin lesion dataset. This gave me another opportunity to follow the process of preparing a model, compiling it, and monitoring its training and validation results.

  Working with EfficientNetV2 helped me understand how models from the same family can still have important differences. We had already trained EfficientNet-B0, so using EfficientNetV2 allowed me to see how newer versions of a model can be designed to improve training speed and efficiency. I also paid closer attention to the accuracy and loss values during training because these results helped us determine whether the model was learning properly or beginning to overfit.

  In the afternoon, we compared EfficientNetV2 with EfficientNet-B0 and ResNet50. We placed the results into the same benchmark-table format so that the models could be evaluated fairly. The table included accuracy, AUC, F1 score, the dataset used, and notes about each model. This helped me understand why researchers should not choose a model based only on its accuracy. Since our dataset contains more images from some lesion classes than others, a high accuracy score may not always mean that the model performs well across every category.

  We also worked on our presentation slides for the Weekly Showcase. Each team member was responsible for an assigned section, and we had to make sure our slides followed the same structure and style. My goal was to present the information clearly without filling the slides with too much text. We later reviewed the presentation as a team and discussed our speaking order, timing, transitions, and possible questions from the audience.

blockers: |
  One challenge was making sure that EfficientNetV2 was trained under conditions that were similar to EfficientNet-B0 and ResNet50. Differences in preprocessing, image size, class labels, or dataset splitting could affect the results and make the comparison less reliable.

  Another challenge was interpreting several evaluation metrics at the same time. Accuracy was easy to understand, but AUC and F1 score provided a more complete view of how the models performed. I had to think carefully about what each result meant, especially because our dataset is imbalanced and some skin lesion classes have fewer images than others.

  Preparing the slides while the model evaluation was still being completed was also challenging. We had to summarize our work before every result was fully finalized. It was important to keep the slides organized, avoid repeating information, and make sure each team member's section connected naturally to the rest of the presentation.

reflection: |
  Today helped me see that model training is only one part of a deep learning project. After a model is trained, the results must be evaluated carefully and compared with other models under similar conditions. Comparing EfficientNetV2, EfficientNet-B0, and ResNet50 gave me a clearer understanding of how different architectures can perform on the same dataset.

  I also learned why it is important to use more than one evaluation metric. Accuracy shows the overall number of correct predictions, but it may hide poor performance on smaller classes. AUC and F1 score helped us look at the results from a broader perspective. This is especially important for DermaBridge because missing a serious skin lesion could have a greater impact than making an incorrect prediction on a more common class.

  Preparing the presentation also reminded me that research must be explained in a way that other people can understand. I had to think about how to turn technical information into clear points without removing the meaning behind the work. Practicing with my team helped me feel more prepared and showed me where our transitions and explanations could be improved.

  Overall, today strengthened my understanding of model comparison, evaluation metrics, teamwork, and research communication. I am becoming more comfortable with following the model-training process and explaining why the results matter to the larger goal of our project.
