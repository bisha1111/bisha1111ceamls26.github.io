what_i_learned: |
  Today marked the beginning of Week 5 of the SAIRI program. We started the morning with an Institute timesheet walkthrough, where we reviewed how to document our hours and activities correctly. This was helpful because accurate time reporting is an important part of remaining organized and accountable throughout the research program.

  We also attended the SAIRI Connect session before moving to the research lab for the afternoon. Once we arrived at the lab, I began with an individual coding period. I used this time to continue working through unfinished parts of the DermaBridge code and focus more closely on problems connected to the different skin-tone data in our project.

  This individual work helped me better understand why fairness must be considered throughout the model-development process. A model may appear successful when looking only at its overall accuracy, but its performance may not be equal across every skin-tone group. Because DermaBridge is intended to support trustworthy skin-lesion screening, we must pay attention to how well the model performs for patients who may be underrepresented in the available datasets.

  The main technical task of the afternoon was tuning EfficientNetV2. Although the model had already begun training, its accuracy still needed improvement. We focused on hyperparameters such as the learning rate, batch size, and number of epochs. I learned that these settings can greatly affect how quickly a model learns, how stable the training process is, and how well it performs on validation data.

  Instead of assuming that the first model configuration was the best one, we prepared to rerun the model with adjusted settings and compare the results. Changing the learning rate can affect the size of each update the model makes during training, while the batch size determines how many images are processed before the model updates its weights. The number of epochs controls how many times the model reviews the full training dataset.

  I also continued working through the Kaggle Python course. The lessons gave me additional practice with Python concepts that are directly useful in our research code. Continuing the tutorials alongside the DermaBridge project helps me strengthen my programming foundation while seeing how those skills are applied in a real machine-learning workflow.

  We also reviewed our progress as we entered Week 5. EfficientNetV2 training had started, the Kaggle Python tutorials were still in progress, and the paper had reached the rough-draft stage for the abstract and literature review. Our next major goals include improving EfficientNetV2 accuracy, updating the benchmark comparison between EfficientNetV2, EfficientNet-B0, and ResNet50, and writing the methods and results sections of the paper.

  Another important focus was the upcoming Midsummer Symposium on June 29. With only one week remaining, we discussed beginning timed pitch practices and preparing our final slides. This reminded me that we must now balance model improvement, paper writing, application development, and presentation preparation within a limited amount of time.

blockers: |
  One challenge today was working with EfficientNetV2 when the initial accuracy still needed improvement. Hyperparameter tuning requires patience because each adjustment must be tested through another training run. It is not enough to change several values randomly, since we must understand what was changed and compare the new results with the earlier model.

  Another challenge was deciding which hyperparameter to adjust first. The learning rate, batch size, and number of epochs can all influence the model, but changing too many settings at the same time can make it difficult to identify which adjustment caused an improvement or decline. We therefore needed to approach the tuning process carefully and record each experiment.

  Working with skin-tone data also remains challenging because the datasets do not represent every group equally. Some skin-tone categories contain fewer examples than others, which may affect how much the model learns about those groups. This means that overall accuracy alone cannot provide a complete picture of whether the model is fair and dependable.

  Time management was another concern because several major responsibilities are happening at once. We need to continue the Kaggle course, improve our models, update the application, write the methods and results sections, and prepare for the symposium. With the presentation only one week away, it is important to make steady progress without rushing through the technical work.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand that model development is an iterative process. Training a model once does not mean the work is complete. When the results are not strong enough, we must examine the configuration, make thoughtful adjustments, rerun the experiment, and compare the new performance with the previous results.

  Hyperparameter tuning also showed me that small technical decisions can have a major effect on a model. Before working on this project, terms such as learning rate, batch size, and epochs seemed like simple settings. I now understand that they influence the way the model learns and must be selected carefully instead of treated as fixed values.

  I also appreciated the opportunity to work independently on the code. Individual coding time allowed me to slow down, examine unfinished sections, and think more carefully about the problems involving different skin-tone groups. This helped me take greater responsibility for understanding the project rather than only following instructions during group sessions.

  The discussion about fairness was especially meaningful because DermaBridge is intended to support people from different backgrounds. A medical AI system should not only perform well on the largest group represented in the dataset. It should also be evaluated carefully to determine whether its predictions remain reliable for groups with fewer available examples.

  Entering Week 5 made the progress of our project feel more real. We now have multiple models to compare, a developing paper, an application in progress, and an upcoming symposium presentation. At the same time, the list of remaining tasks showed me how much work still needs to be completed before the project is ready to present.

  Preparing for the Midsummer Symposium will require both technical understanding and strong communication. We must be able to explain why we selected our models, how we trained them, what the results show, and how fairness and explainability are included in the DermaBridge project. Practicing our pitch under a time limit will help us communicate these ideas more clearly and confidently.

  Overall, Day 20 strengthened my understanding of EfficientNetV2, hyperparameter tuning, fairness evaluation, independent problem-solving, and research planning. It also reminded me that improvement comes from testing, comparing, documenting, and learning from every result rather than expecting the first attempt to be perfect.
