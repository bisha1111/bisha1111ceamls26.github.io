what_i_learned: |
  Today, I stepped outside my regular research environment and attended the AWS Summit in Washington, D.C. The experience gave me a closer look at how artificial intelligence and cloud computing are being used beyond textbooks and research papers to address real challenges across different industries.

  As I explored the summit, I visited booths from companies such as Datadog, GitLab, Commvault, AMD, and several others. Instead of simply collecting brochures, I challenged myself to ask questions about each company’s technology, workplace culture, internship opportunities, and career paths in software engineering and AI. These conversations helped me understand that there are many possible directions within the technology industry and that every company approaches innovation differently.

  One of my goals for the summit was to make 20 new LinkedIn connections. I finished the day with approximately 10 connections from different companies and universities. Although I did not reach my numerical goal, the conversations I had were genuine and valuable. I learned that networking is not about adding as many names as possible; it is about creating meaningful connections that can grow over time.

  A major highlight of the summit was attending the breakout session titled "Building an AI-Ready University: From Campus to Career Readiness." The session explored how colleges and universities can prepare students for a future in which artificial intelligence will influence nearly every profession.

  I learned about the AI Readiness Maturity Model, which describes how institutions can progress from small, experimental uses of AI to full institutional transformation. The session emphasized that becoming AI-ready requires more than purchasing new software. Universities must develop responsible policies, train faculty and students, encourage innovation, and create a culture in which AI is used ethically and effectively.

blockers: |
  I did not experience any major challenges during the summit. My only disappointment was not achieving my goal of making 20 LinkedIn connections.

  However, reflecting on the experience helped me recognize that the quality of a connection matters more than the quantity. I would rather leave with 10 meaningful conversations than 20 connections with people I barely spoke to. This realization changed the way I view professional networking and encouraged me to focus more on listening, asking thoughtful questions, and building genuine relationships.

reflection: |
  Attending the AWS Summit was one of the most inspiring professional development experiences of my internship so far. Walking through the exhibit hall and seeing companies demonstrate their technologies made the future of AI and cloud computing feel more real and accessible.

  The summit also reminded me that success in technology requires more than knowing how to code or train a model. It requires curiosity, communication, responsibility, and the ability to understand how technology affects people, organizations, and communities.

  The breakout session especially made me reflect on the role universities play in preparing students for an AI-driven workforce. As a computer science student and AI researcher, I want to develop strong technical skills, but I also want to understand how those skills can be used responsibly and in ways that create meaningful impact.

  I left Washington, D.C., with new knowledge, new professional connections, and a renewed sense of motivation. Today showed me that every conversation, session, and experience can open a door. My responsibility is to continue learning, remain curious, and be prepared when those doors open.
