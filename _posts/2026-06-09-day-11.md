what_i_learned: |
  Today was a calm but informative day that allowed me to improve both my research
  writing and technical skills. We began with a session led by Dr. Pandey, where
  we continued learning about literature reviews and how to make our academic
  writing clearer and more connected.

  One of the main topics we discussed was the use of transition words in different
  sections of a research paper. I learned that transitions are important because
  they help ideas flow naturally from one sentence or paragraph to the next.
  Depending on the purpose of the sentence, a writer may need a transition that
  introduces a similar idea, provides an example, shows a contrast, or presents a
  conclusion.

  We also discussed how to maintain our own voice while citing information from
  other authors. Before this session, I sometimes thought that using research from
  another source meant allowing that author's words to control the paragraph.
  However, I learned that the source should support my point rather than replace
  my own explanation. I should introduce the idea, use evidence from the source,
  and then explain how that evidence connects to the DermaBridge project.

  Dr. Pandey also used a game to help us practice selecting the correct transition
  words for different situations. The activity made the lesson more engaging and
  gave me an opportunity to apply what we had discussed. It helped me see how one
  transition word can completely change the relationship between two ideas.

  Later, we joined our DermaBridge research group and learned about convolutional
  neural networks, also known as CNNs. We discussed how CNNs are designed to
  process images and recognize visual patterns such as edges, shapes, colors, and
  textures. This is especially relevant to our project because our deep-learning
  model will need to examine skin lesion images and learn patterns connected to
  different diagnoses.

  We also learned about encoding and why it is necessary when working with
  categorical data. Machine-learning models generally work with numerical values,
  so text categories such as diagnosis names, sex, or lesion locations must be
  converted into a numerical form before the model can use them.

  We read two articles that explained CNNs and encoding techniques before beginning
  our practical work. Blessing introduced us to label encoding, frequency encoding,
  and one-hot encoding. Each member of the group was assigned an encoding method
  to study and explain. I focused on label encoding, which assigns a different
  number to each category in a column.

  Label encoding felt familiar because we had already used it while working with
  our dataset the previous day. At that time, I understood what the code was doing,
  but I did not know the official name of the method. Learning the correct term
  helped me connect the code I had written to the larger machine-learning concept.

  Blessing explained each technique carefully and then asked us to describe the
  concepts in our own words. This was helpful because it required me to understand
  the idea instead of simply repeating a definition from an article. By the end
  of the session, I understood why different encoding methods may be more suitable
  for different types of data.

blockers: |
  Surprisingly, I did not experience any major blockers today. The lessons were
  presented clearly, and our group was able to complete everything on the to-do
  list without running into serious coding errors or delays.

  Some parts of the literature review session became a little difficult to remain
  focused on because we spent a long time discussing writing concepts. However,
  the transition-word game helped me become engaged again and made it easier to
  remember the lesson.

  Although label encoding was fairly easy for me to understand, I still need to
  spend more time reviewing frequency encoding and one-hot encoding. I understand
  their basic purposes, but I want to become more confident about deciding which
  method should be used for a particular column in a dataset.

reflection: |
  Today was a relaxed and productive day. I appreciated having a day where I could
  learn new concepts without dealing with many technical problems. It allowed me
  to focus more on understanding the material instead of spending most of my time
  correcting errors.

  The session with Dr. Pandey helped me recognize that strong research writing
  requires more than collecting sources and placing citations into a paper. I must
  guide the reader through my ideas, connect the evidence to my argument, and make
  sure that my own voice remains present throughout the literature review.

  I also enjoyed the time spent with my research group because the lessons on CNNs
  and encoding were directly connected to the work we are doing with DermaBridge.
  Understanding CNNs gave me a clearer picture of how a model can learn from skin
  lesion images, while encoding showed me how patient and diagnosis information
  can be prepared for machine-learning models.

  I was especially interested to discover that I had already used label encoding
  without knowing its name. This reminded me that I am learning more than I
  sometimes realize. As I continue working on the project, I am beginning to
  recognize the concepts behind the code rather than only following instructions.

  Our group completed everything we planned without any major setbacks, which made
  the day feel successful. I ended the session feeling more confident about both
  the writing and technical sides of our research, and I am looking forward to
  applying these concepts as we continue developing DermaBridge.
