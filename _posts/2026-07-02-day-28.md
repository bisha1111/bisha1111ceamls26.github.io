what_i_learned: |
  Today, our team continued the exploratory data analysis of the new skin lesion datasets we identified. We examined each dataset’s size, class distribution, missing values, diagnosis labels, and available skin-tone information. This helped us determine which datasets could strengthen our project and which ones might create difficulties during integration.

  We documented our findings in a shared Google Drive table so that the team could compare the new datasets with the ones already included in our preprocessing pipeline. I learned that a dataset should not be selected only because it contains many images. Its label quality, demographic information, accessibility, and compatibility with the existing diagnosis categories are equally important.

  In the afternoon, we attended a DNA lecture led by Dr. Saroj K. Pramanik. The session encouraged us to connect our dataset findings with the broader goals of the research project and to think carefully about how every technical decision contributes to the reliability of the final system.

  We later worked in Overleaf to review the citations in our abstract, introduction, and literature review. We checked that claims based on previous research were properly supported, every in-text citation matched an entry in the reference list, and the IEEE citation format was used consistently. During the group citation review, each team member examined a section of the paper and identified missing or unclear references.

blockers: |
  One challenge was comparing datasets that used different diagnosis categories, file structures, and methods for reporting patient information. Some datasets also mentioned diversity but did not provide enough skin-tone metadata for a detailed fairness analysis.

  Citation work was also time-consuming because every source had to be verified and connected to the correct statement. We had to ensure that no reference appeared without being cited and that no citation was missing from the reference list.

reflection: |
  Today showed me that exploratory data analysis and academic citation serve a similar purpose: both make research more trustworthy. EDA helps us understand the evidence contained in our datasets, while citations show where our scientific claims and ideas originated.

  I also learned that strong research requires more than writing technical explanations. Every dataset choice, comparison, and claim must be supported clearly enough for another researcher to review and understand. By the end of the day, our dataset documentation and paper were more organized, transparent, and prepared for the next stage of the project.
