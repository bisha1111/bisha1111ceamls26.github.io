what_i_learned: |
  Today, we were introduced to computer vision, and it became one of my favorite
  workshops in the program so far. Daniel guided us through using YOLO and OpenCV
  to detect objects in both images and videos. The workshop was very interactive,
  and I enjoyed being able to immediately test the tools instead of only learning
  about them through explanations.

  During the workshop, I experimented with object detection using the Michael
  Jackson "Thriller" music video and a poster from one of my favorite television
  shows, 6teen. It was interesting to watch the model identify people and different
  objects in each frame. This helped me understand how computer vision systems
  process visual information and make predictions about what appears in an image
  or video.

  We also learned about Teachable Machine and how image-recognition models are
  trained using examples from different categories. I created my own model and
  trained it to recognize candles, eyeglass cases, and charger blocks. Collecting
  the images and testing the model showed me how the amount, quality, background,
  and variety of training data can affect the model's accuracy. One participant
  even trained a model using her dog, which made the activity funny and memorable.

  The workshop helped me better understand that a model does not automatically
  know what an object is. It must first be given enough labeled examples so that
  it can learn patterns such as shapes, colors, and textures. I also saw how a
  model can become confused when objects look similar or when the lighting and
  background change.

blockers: |
  My main challenge today was getting my images and videos to load and run properly
  in Google Colab. The video I selected took nearly twenty minutes to process,
  which caused me to fall behind during part of the workshop. It was frustrating
  because the rest of the group had already moved on while I was still waiting for
  the output.

  Once the video finished processing, I had to work quickly to catch up and make
  sure I understood the steps I had missed. This experience taught me that larger
  files may require more processing time and computing power. In the future, I
  will try using shorter videos, smaller file sizes, or lower resolutions while I
  am testing my code so that I can receive results faster.

reflection: |
  Today was a very good day, and I genuinely enjoyed learning about computer
  vision. Out of all the workshops we have completed so far, this one felt the most
  connected to the technical direction of the DermaBridge project. It allowed me
  to see how artificial intelligence can examine visual information instead of
  only working with numbers or written data.

  Computer vision will be an important part of DermaBridge because our application
  will need to analyze photographs of skin lesions provided by users. Although
  detecting a skin lesion is more complex than identifying everyday objects, the
  concepts we learned today helped me understand the foundation behind image
  classification and object detection. Our model will need to identify meaningful
  patterns in skin lesion images and use those patterns to support a careful risk
  assessment.

  The workshop also reminded me why the quality of our dataset matters. If the
  training images do not include enough variety in skin tones, lesion types,
  lighting conditions, and image quality, the model may not perform equally well
  for every user. This connects directly to our goal of building a trustworthy and
  fair system instead of focusing only on overall accuracy.

  I also enjoyed watching the weekly presentations because they gave me a chance
  to learn about the other research groups and the problems they are working to
  solve. Seeing their progress made me appreciate how many different areas of
  artificial intelligence are being explored throughout the program. I left today
  excited to continue learning about computer vision and eventually apply these
  skills to the development of DermaBridge.
