what_i_learned: |
  Today, our team focused on making the DermaBridge application easier to understand and navigate. Before adding more technical features, we considered what a first-time user would need to see and how each screen should guide them through the app.

  We examined several existing healthcare applications to learn how they present medical information and organize their interfaces. This research helped us make decisions about the layout, navigation, and overall appearance of DermaBridge.

  One of our main improvements was creating an introductory sequence for new users. These screens briefly explain the purpose of the application, the importance of fair screening across different skin tones, and the option of connecting users with professional dermatology care. Users can then sign in, register, or explore the app without an account.

  We also reorganized parts of the code by developing shared interface elements that can be used across different pages. This helped maintain a consistent appearance throughout the application.

  In addition, we added accessibility descriptions for screen readers and improved how users move between screens. We also created the first version of the image-capture process. The AI model is not connected yet, but users will eventually use this feature to photograph a skin concern for analysis.

blockers: |
  There were no significant blockers today. Our main focus was refining the app’s structure and completing features that will support the future integration of the machine learning model.

reflection: |
  Today really showed me that creating a healthcare application involves more than developing a working model. The design must also be clear, accessible, and comfortable for users who may already be concerned about their health.

  By improving the introduction, navigation, accessibility, and camera process, we made important progress toward turning DermaBridge into a practical and welcoming screening application.
