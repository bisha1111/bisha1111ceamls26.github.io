what_i_learned: |
  Today was a long and demanding day, but I learned a lot about both the writing
  and technical sides of research. We began by learning more about literature
  reviews and how to create titles that are clear, interesting, and connected to
  the main focus of a research paper. I learned that a strong title should give
  the reader an idea of what the paper is about without being too broad or
  unnecessarily complicated.

  We also reviewed the PEACC checklist and discussed how it can help us evaluate
  information before including it in our research. Going through the checklist
  again helped me understand the importance of checking the purpose, evidence,
  authority, currency, and context of a source. This was useful because not every
  article found online is reliable or relevant enough to support an academic
  research project.

  After reviewing the checklist, we studied the general structure of a literature
  review. We discussed how to organize information by common themes instead of
  simply summarizing one article after another. I learned that a literature review
  should explain how different studies connect, where researchers agree or
  disagree, and what gaps still exist in the field. We also reviewed ways to revise
  our writing by checking the organization, clarity, grammar, transitions, and
  connection between each source and our research topic.

  Later, I joined my DermaBridge research group and continued exploring our skin
  lesion datasets with Python. Blessing guided us through the process of examining
  the dataset and breaking it down into smaller sections so that we could better
  understand the information it contained. We looked at different columns, checked
  the organization of the data, and began analyzing patterns related to the skin
  lesion images and patient information.

  This activity helped me understand that dataset exploration is an important step
  before training any machine-learning model. We cannot simply place data into a
  model without first understanding its structure, identifying missing values,
  checking the different categories, and looking for possible imbalances. Since
  DermaBridge is focused on trustworthy and fair skin lesion screening, it is
  especially important for us to know whether certain diagnoses, skin tones, or
  patient groups are underrepresented.

  We also continued reading research papers related to artificial intelligence,
  dermatology, skin lesion classification, medical image datasets, and fairness.
  These papers will help us complete our literature review assignment and better
  explain why the DermaBridge project is needed. By the end of the day, I had a
  stronger understanding of how the information from previous studies connects to
  the dataset analysis and model development we are doing as a team.

blockers: |
  My main challenge today was keeping up during the dataset exploration session.
  Blessing was guiding us through several Python steps, and I experienced errors
  even though I was following the instructions carefully. At times, one small
  mistake in the code caused the entire cell to stop working, and it was not always
  easy for me to immediately identify the problem.

  I had to ask for help periodically so that I could correct the errors and continue
  with the rest of the group. Although this was frustrating, it reminded me that
  debugging is a normal part of programming. I am learning that I need to slow
  down, read the error messages carefully, and check details such as spelling,
  variable names, parentheses, file paths, and indentation before assuming that
  the entire code is wrong.

  Another challenge was staying focused after spending so many hours looking at my
  computer screen. My eyes began to hurt, and I developed a headache from reading,
  writing, and coding for most of the day. I had to take a few short breaks to rest
  my eyes before returning to the work. Moving forward, I need to be more
  intentional about taking short breaks, adjusting my screen brightness, and giving
  myself time to reset during long research sessions.
