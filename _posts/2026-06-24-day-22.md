what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on data analysis, paper writing, and preparation for the Midsummer Symposium. We began the morning with the institute-led Intro to Data Analysis Part 2 session in Graves Business School Room 112. The session helped me better understand how researchers organize, interpret, and communicate data instead of only presenting raw numbers. I connected many of the concepts from the session to our DermaBridge dataset, preprocessing pipeline, and model-evaluation process.

  One important lesson was that data analysis is not only about calculating values. It also involves checking whether the results make sense, identifying patterns, considering possible errors, and understanding how the quality of the dataset can affect the final conclusions. This connects directly to our project because the skin-lesion datasets contain class imbalance, missing information, and unequal amounts of data across diagnosis categories.

  During the afternoon, we worked on the results section of our research paper. Our goal was to present and interpret the performance of the three deep-learning models we tested: EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, and EfficientNetV2. We discussed creating a benchmark table that includes the accuracy, AUC, and F1 score for each model so that their performance can be compared clearly.

  I learned that accuracy shows the overall percentage of predictions the model classified correctly, but it may not provide a complete picture when the dataset is imbalanced. A model may perform well on the most common class and still struggle with less common lesion types. AUC helps measure how well the model separates classes, while the F1 score balances precision and recall. Looking at all three metrics gives a stronger understanding of model performance than relying on accuracy alone.

  We also discussed using confusion matrices to identify where each model is failing. A confusion matrix can show which lesion categories are being confused with one another and whether a model is struggling with certain classes. This is especially important in medical imaging because some errors may be more serious than others. For example, failing to identify a potentially malignant lesion could be more harmful than confusing two less serious categories.

  Another important part of the results section was comparing the models and explaining which one performed best. We cannot make that decision using only one score. We also need to consider validation performance, training behavior, efficiency, and the types of mistakes each model made. The written interpretation should explain the results carefully without making claims that the data does not support.

  We also worked on identifying honest limitations. Although the models can be compared using accuracy, AUC, F1 score, and confusion matrices, these results do not yet show whether the models perform equally well across different skin tones. The PAD-UFES-20 dataset includes Fitzpatrick skin-type information, but HAM10000 does not. Because of this, the fairness gap has not yet been fully measured and must be clearly acknowledged in the paper.

  After a short break, we refined the literature review by tightening the language, removing repeated ideas, checking for citation gaps, and making sure each source connected to the main theme of the paper. We focused on aligning the review with topics such as deep learning, skin-lesion classification, multimodal data, fairness, explainability, and trustworthy artificial intelligence.

  Later, we prepared for the Midsummer Symposium by reviewing slides and practicing our speaking sections. During the pitch session, I practiced with a partner and received feedback on my pacing, content, clarity, and timing. This helped me recognize that I need to slow down when speaking, pause after important points, and explain technical terms in a way that is easier for the audience to understand.

blockers: |
  One challenge is making sure the results for EfficientNet-B0, ResNet50, and EfficientNetV2 are organized in the same format before comparing them. The accuracy, AUC, and F1 scores must come from consistent evaluation conditions so that the comparison is fair.

  Another challenge is interpreting the confusion matrices correctly. We need to identify the classes each model struggled with and explain why those errors matter instead of only reporting the total number of mistakes.

  The fairness analysis is also incomplete because skin-tone information is not available for every image in the combined dataset. We therefore cannot yet claim that the models perform equally well across all skin-tone groups.

  I also need to continue improving my speaking pace. I sometimes talk too quickly when I am nervous, which can make my explanation difficult to follow.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand that a strong results section is more than a table of numbers. It should explain what the metrics mean, compare the strengths and weaknesses of the models, identify where errors occur, and acknowledge the limits of the current findings.

  I also learned that high accuracy does not automatically mean that a model is reliable or fair. It is important to examine AUC, F1 score, confusion matrices, class imbalance, and group-level performance before making strong conclusions. This is especially important for DermaBridge because the project involves medical images and aims to support trustworthy skin-lesion screening.

  Refining the literature review helped me see how previous research should connect directly to our own project. Each source should help explain what has already been studied, what problems remain, and why our research is important.

  The presentation practice was also useful because it gave me a chance to improve how I communicate technical information. I need to slow down, avoid reading directly from the slides, and explain the project in a clear and confident way.

  Overall, today was productive because we made progress on the paper, results interpretation, literature review, and symposium presentation. I am becoming more confident in understanding model-evaluation metrics and explaining our research findings honestly and clearly.
