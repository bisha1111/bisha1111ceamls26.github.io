what_i_learned: |
  Today was focused on deep learning architecture, comparative model evaluation, and preparation for our upcoming DermaBridge Weekly Showcase. In the morning, we began implementing EfficientNetV2, which was the third deep learning architecture included in our model comparison. We first reviewed the structure of the model, prepared the architecture, and made sure it could work with the image size, class labels, and training process we had already established for the project.

  Working with EfficientNetV2 helped me understand that even models from the same family can have important architectural differences. We had previously worked with EfficientNet-B0, but EfficientNetV2 was introduced as a more recent architecture designed to improve training speed and computational efficiency while maintaining strong performance. This allowed me to see how deep learning models continue to develop over time and why researchers may test newer architectures rather than relying only on older baseline models.

  After completing the initial setup, we began training and validating EfficientNetV2 on our skin lesion dataset. I observed how the model processed the training images across different epochs and how the training and validation metrics changed over time. This helped reinforce my understanding of accuracy and loss graphs. Training accuracy showed how well the model was learning from the data it had already seen, while validation accuracy helped us understand how well the model performed on unseen data.

  I also learned that the relationship between training and validation results can provide important information about model performance. If training accuracy continues to improve while validation accuracy remains low or begins to decrease, the model may be overfitting. If both values remain low, the model may not be learning enough from the data. Monitoring both results is therefore necessary when deciding whether the training process is effective.

  In the afternoon, we moved into comparative evaluation. Our goal was to compare EfficientNetV2 with the two models we had already trained: EfficientNet-B0 and ResNet50. Instead of looking at the models separately, we organized their results side by side. This made it easier to evaluate how each architecture performed under similar conditions and determine which model may be more suitable for our DermaBridge skin lesion classification system.

  We used a consistent benchmark-table format that included the model name, dataset, accuracy, AUC, F1 score, and additional notes. I learned that maintaining the same format across all models is important because comparisons can become misleading if each model is evaluated using different criteria. Keeping the dataset, preprocessing steps, class labels, and evaluation metrics consistent gives us a stronger basis for deciding which model performs best.

  Accuracy was one of the main metrics we reviewed, but we also discussed why accuracy alone may not be sufficient for medical image classification. Our merged dataset contains more examples of some skin lesion classes than others, especially benign lesions such as nevi. Because of this imbalance, a model could achieve a high accuracy score by performing well on the majority class while still struggling with less common but medically important classes.

  AUC and F1 score provided additional information about the models. AUC helped us examine how well a model could distinguish between classes, while the F1 score helped balance precision and recall. This is especially important in a medical screening project because both false positives and false negatives can affect how useful the model is. A false negative could cause a potentially dangerous lesion to be overlooked, while too many false positives could lead to unnecessary concern or referrals.

  Later in the afternoon, we began building the presentation slides for Thursday's Weekly Showcase. Since Friday would be Juneteenth and there would be no institute session, our slide-building schedule was moved to Wednesday. Each student was assigned a specific section of the presentation and was responsible for completing that section before the end of the day.

  Preparing the slides required us to summarize our recent progress, including the models we trained, the datasets we used, the evaluation metrics we reviewed, and the next steps for the project. I learned that presentation slides should not contain every technical detail from the research. Instead, they should highlight the most important information and support the speaker's explanation. This required us to decide which graphs, results, and short descriptions would help the audience understand our work without making the presentation feel overcrowded.

  The slide-building process also helped me recognize the importance of consistency in a team presentation. Even though each student worked on a different section, all slides needed to follow the same visual style, terminology, formatting, and level of detail. The final presentation should feel like one complete project rather than several unrelated sections created by different people.

blockers: |
  One challenge was implementing EfficientNetV2 while keeping the training setup consistent with EfficientNet-B0 and ResNet50. For a fair comparison, the models should use similar preprocessing steps, image sizes, dataset splits, class labels, and evaluation methods. Even a small difference in one of these areas could affect the results and make it difficult to determine whether one architecture was actually better than another.

  Another challenge was monitoring the training and validation process. Deep learning models can take a long time to train, and the results may change from one epoch to another. We had to pay close attention to the accuracy and loss values to determine whether the model was improving, overfitting, or struggling to learn from the dataset.

  Comparing the three models was also challenging because no single metric provides a complete explanation of model performance. Accuracy may appear strong even when a model performs poorly on minority classes. AUC and F1 score provide more information, but they also need to be interpreted carefully. I had to think about what each metric measured and why it was relevant to a medical image classification system.

  The imbalance in the skin lesion dataset created another difficulty. Some lesion categories had many more images than others, which could influence the model's predictions and overall evaluation results. This made it necessary to look beyond the overall accuracy and consider whether the model was learning to recognize the less common classes.

  Preparing the slides within the available time was also challenging. We had to continue working on model training and evaluation while also organizing the results for the presentation. Turning detailed technical work into short, understandable slide content required careful decision-making because there was not enough space to include every part of the process.

  Another challenge was making sure that the slides created by different team members remained consistent. Each student had a separate responsibility, but the presentation still needed to use the same formatting, terminology, and structure throughout. This required communication and attention to how each slide connected to the next section.

reflection: |
  Today showed me that choosing a model for a research project requires more than training several architectures and selecting the one with the highest accuracy. A fair model comparison depends on using the same dataset, preprocessing methods, training conditions, and evaluation metrics. Without this consistency, the results may not accurately show which model is performing better.

  Implementing EfficientNetV2 also expanded my understanding of deep learning architecture. Before this project, I mainly understood model names as separate options, but I am beginning to see how each architecture is designed with different goals. ResNet50 uses residual connections to support deeper networks, EfficientNet-B0 balances network depth, width, and image resolution, and EfficientNetV2 builds on that approach with improvements intended to make training faster and more efficient.

  Comparing these models helped me think more critically about the purpose of evaluation metrics. Accuracy is useful, but it does not always tell the full story, especially when working with an imbalanced medical dataset. A model may appear successful overall while still failing to recognize an important lesion category. AUC and F1 score helped provide a broader view of performance and reminded me that evaluation should reflect the real purpose of the system.

  This is especially important for DermaBridge because our goal is not simply to build a model that produces a high score. We are working toward a trustworthy screening and referral system that can support early skin lesion detection. For that reason, the model must perform reliably across different lesion types and patient groups. The results must also be interpreted carefully before the system can be considered useful in a healthcare setting.

  The presentation preparation gave me another perspective on the research process. Researchers must not only complete technical work but also explain that work clearly to other people. A strong presentation should help the audience understand the problem, the methods, the results, and the importance of the project without requiring them to have an advanced background in deep learning.

  I also learned that teamwork becomes especially important when preparing a shared presentation. Each person's section must connect naturally with the others, and everyone must understand the overall direction of the project. Even when tasks are divided, the team is still responsible for producing one complete and organized final presentation.

  Overall, today strengthened my understanding of model architecture, comparative evaluation, medical image classification, and research communication. It helped me see how model training, performance analysis, and presentation preparation all connect. The work we completed today prepared us for the next stage of the project and for presenting our progress during the Weekly Showcase.
