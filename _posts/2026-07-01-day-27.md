what_i_learned: |
  Today, our team focused on strengthening the methodology and data foundation of our skin cancer detection project. We began with a writing session in Overleaf, where each team member worked on an assigned part of the methodology section. I was responsible for writing the preprocessing pipeline.

  In my section, I explained how the images were prepared before model training. This included converting the images to RGB format, resizing them to 224 by 224 pixels, normalizing the image values, and applying augmentation methods such as flipping, rotation, and blurring. I also documented how we handled missing clinical information, including replacing missing ages with the median value and labeling missing sex or lesion-location information as unknown.

  Later, we researched additional skin cancer datasets that could improve the diversity of our project. We examined sources such as the ISIC Archive, SD-198, ASAN, and SkinCon. We compared their sizes, diagnosis categories, accessibility, and the amount of information they provided about different skin tones and patient demographics.

  During the exploratory data analysis session, we checked the structure of the datasets, class distributions, missing values, and label formats. We also considered whether the diagnosis labels could be aligned with the categories already being used in our project.

  In the final session, we researched fairness metrics beyond the Fitzpatrick skin-type scale. I learned about Equalized Odds, Demographic Parity, Equal Opportunity, and Predictive Parity. These measurements can help us evaluate whether a model performs consistently across groups based on skin tone, age, or sex instead of relying only on overall accuracy.

blockers: |
  The main challenge was finding datasets that included reliable skin-tone and demographic information. Some datasets contained many images but lacked the metadata needed to compare model performance across different groups.

  Another challenge was that the datasets did not always use the same diagnosis labels. Before combining them, we would need to carefully map their categories to avoid introducing incorrect or inconsistent labels into the project.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand that preprocessing is one of the most important parts of machine learning research. A model may have a strong architecture, but its performance still depends heavily on how the data is cleaned, organized, and prepared.

  Writing the preprocessing section also showed me the importance of documenting every technical decision clearly. Research should be detailed enough for another person to understand the process and reproduce the work.

  The fairness discussion was especially meaningful because medical AI should work well for everyone, not only for the groups that appear most often in the training data. I ended the day with a stronger understanding that building a trustworthy model requires accuracy, diverse data, clear documentation, and careful evaluation across different populations.
