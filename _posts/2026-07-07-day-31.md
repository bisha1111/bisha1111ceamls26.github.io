what_i_learned: |
  Today, our team focused on a research deep dive about model performance in the DermaBridge skin cancer detection project. The main research question was why a model can show high precision but still have low validation accuracy. This was important because high precision means that when the model predicts a class, it is often correct, but low validation accuracy means the model may still be missing many samples or performing poorly across other classes.

  My pair focused on class imbalance, data distribution shifts, and dataset bias. I learned that class imbalance can create a misleading performance picture because the model may learn the majority class very well while struggling with minority diagnosis categories. In a skin lesion dataset, if one class such as nevus appears much more often than melanoma, basal cell carcinoma, or dermatofibroma, the model may become confident in the dominant class while failing to generalize across the full set of diagnoses.

  I also learned that data distribution shift can hurt validation accuracy when the training data and validation data do not look the same. For example, differences in image quality, lighting, camera type, lesion location, skin tone, or clinical setting can make the model perform well on one group of images but poorly on another. This connects directly to our project because we are combining datasets from different sources, and each dataset may have its own patterns.

  Dataset bias was another major point. If the dataset does not represent all skin tones, lesion types, age groups, and clinical conditions fairly, the model may learn patterns that work for some groups but fail for others. This is especially serious in healthcare AI because a model that performs unevenly can reduce trust and create unequal outcomes for patients. Since DermaBridge is focused on explainable and fair skin lesion screening, understanding bias is not optional. It is part of making the system responsible.

  I also completed Course 4 from Google for Developers’ Machine Learning Crash Course on fairness. This course helped me understand that fairness in machine learning is not just about getting a high model score. It is about checking whether the model performs fairly across different groups and whether the training data includes enough representation. This connected strongly to DermaBridge because skin cancer detection models can behave differently across skin tones if the dataset is imbalanced or biased. The course helped me think more clearly about fairness evaluation, hidden bias, and why healthcare AI systems need to be tested beyond overall accuracy.

  During the research session, we also reviewed papers connected to object detection, dermatology datasets, skin-tone classification, and model training strategies. These readings helped us connect our model results to existing research instead of only guessing from our own experiment. I learned that strong model improvement should be based on evidence, not just trial and error.

  In the afternoon, each pair presented their findings. Our group explained how class imbalance, dataset bias, and distribution shifts can cause the gap between precision and validation accuracy. We also discussed possible fixes such as balanced sampling, stronger data augmentation, class weighting, better train-validation splitting, fairness evaluation by Fitzpatrick skin type, and reviewing the dataset before model training. The goal was not to code today, but to reason carefully and create a clear improvement plan before making changes.

blockers: |
  One challenge today was understanding why high precision does not automatically mean the model is strong overall. At first, it is easy to think that high precision means the model is performing well, but precision only tells part of the story. A model can be precise for the cases it predicts while still missing many correct cases or failing across minority classes.

  Another blocker was separating the different causes of poor validation accuracy. Class imbalance, data distribution shift, and dataset bias are connected, but they are not the same thing. Class imbalance is about uneven class counts, distribution shift is about differences between training and validation data, and dataset bias is about unfair or incomplete representation in the data. Understanding these differences helped me explain the problem more clearly.

  It was also challenging to connect the Google fairness course and the research papers to our own project in a practical way. Some ideas were general machine learning concepts, while others applied directly to healthcare AI and skin lesion detection. I had to think carefully about how fairness, bias, and representation connect to our model’s precision and validation accuracy gap.

  Another challenge was deciding which fixes should come first. There are many possible solutions, but not all of them should be implemented at the same time. We needed to choose the fixes that are most likely to improve validation accuracy, reduce bias, and support fairness without making the pipeline too complicated.

reflection: |
  Today helped me understand that model performance is more than one number. Accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, AUC, confusion matrices, and fairness metrics all tell different parts of the story. A model with high precision can still be unreliable if it performs poorly on underrepresented classes or fails on images that look different from the training data.

  I also realized that research is not only about building a model quickly. Sometimes the most important work is slowing down to ask why the model behaves the way it does. Before changing the code, we need to understand the root cause of the problem. This makes the improvement process more thoughtful and prevents us from making random changes that may not solve the real issue.

  The Google fairness course made me think more deeply about responsible AI. It showed me that fairness has to be measured and documented, not just mentioned in the paper. For DermaBridge, this means we need to look beyond overall accuracy and ask whether the model is performing well across different diagnosis categories and skin-tone groups.

  The discussion on class imbalance and dataset bias connected strongly to the fairness goals of DermaBridge. Since skin cancer detection can affect real patients, the model should not only perform well on the most common images in the dataset. It should be tested across diagnosis categories and skin-tone groups so that we can identify where it is strong and where it still needs improvement.

  By the end of the day, I felt more confident explaining the precision and validation accuracy gap. I understood that the gap may come from uneven class distribution, biased data, weak representation of darker skin tones, or differences between training and validation images. This gave our team a stronger foundation for deciding what to fix next.

  Overall, Day 31 showed me that responsible AI research requires both technical skill and careful reasoning. The goal is not just to make the model look good on paper, but to make it more reliable, fair, and useful for the people it is meant to help.
