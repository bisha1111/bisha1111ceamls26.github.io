what_i_learned: |
  Today was one of our most hands-on and intensive research sessions so far. We successfully trained our first deep learning model, EfficientNet-B0, and began training a ResNet50 model for comparison. This helped me better understand how computer vision models can be applied to skin lesion classification and how different model architectures may perform on the same dataset.

  Throughout the training process, I learned how to read and interpret model performance using accuracy and loss graphs. These graphs helped me see whether the model was learning effectively and whether there were possible issues such as low performance or overfitting. Seeing the results visually made the training process feel more practical and easier to understand.

  My graduate mentor also included several TODO sections in the code for us to complete. This allowed me to follow along with the implementation, gain hands-on experience, and understand how the training pipeline works. Instead of just running the code, I was able to actively participate in the process and learn the reasoning behind each step.

  During training, our first model did not perform as well as we expected. We investigated the issue and worked on adjusting hyperparameters to improve the results. This helped me understand how important hyperparameter tuning is in deep learning and how small changes can affect model performance. We also took a break from coding and participated in a fun group activity on Gartic Phone, which gave us a chance to relax, laugh with other interns, and recharge before returning to our research work.

blockers: |
  One challenge we encountered was that our initial model performance was not as strong as expected. However, this became a valuable learning experience because it pushed us to explore hyperparameter adjustments, review the training process more carefully, and discuss ways to improve the model's results.

reflection: |
  Today was challenging, but it was also one of the most rewarding days of the program so far. For the first time, I felt deeply involved in the model training process instead of only learning about machine learning concepts in theory. Being able to interpret training metrics, modify code, and troubleshoot model performance helped me gain more confidence in my understanding of deep learning.

  Working with EfficientNet-B0 and ResNet50 also helped me see how computer vision research is built through testing, comparison, and improvement. Training a model is not just about getting a result; it is about understanding what the result means and using that information to make better decisions.

  Although training deep learning models can be complex, I enjoyed the challenge. Seeing our work move closer to a functioning skin lesion classification system made the research feel even more meaningful. This project continues to show me how artificial intelligence can be used in healthcare, especially when the goal is to build tools that are more accurate, trustworthy, and useful for real people.
