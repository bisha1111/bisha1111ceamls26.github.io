what_i_learned: |
  Today gave us an opportunity to experience what a full in-person research day
  would feel like. Instead of moving between several general workshops, we spent
  most of the day working independently with our DermaBridge research group. This
  gave us more time to focus on our project, collaborate with one another, and work
  through the technical tasks at a more realistic research pace.

  One of our main goals was to learn how to combine multiple skin lesion datasets.
  We worked with the HAM10000 and PAD-UFES-20 datasets, which contain different
  types of information about skin lesions and patients. Before combining them, we
  had to examine their columns, understand how the diagnoses were labeled, and
  determine which information could be matched across both datasets.

  I learned that merging datasets involves much more than placing two files
  together. The diagnosis labels, column names, image paths, and missing values
  must be reviewed carefully. If similar information is written differently in
  each dataset, it must be standardized before the data can be used to train a
  model. This helped me understand why data preparation is one of the most
  important and time-consuming parts of a machine-learning project.

  After working on the merged dataset, we practiced dividing the data into training
  and testing portions. We then began training a machine-learning model to classify
  the different skin lesion diagnoses. This activity showed me how the model learns
  from one section of the data and is later evaluated using examples that it did
  not see during training. It also helped me understand why testing the model on
  separate data is necessary for determining whether it can make useful predictions
  instead of simply memorizing the training examples.

  Dr. Pramanik also held an informative session with our group about skin cancer
  terminology and the different categories of skin lesions included in our
  datasets. He explained the characteristics of several types of skin cancer and
  showed us examples of how they may appear across different skin tones. Seeing
  these examples was very important because the same condition may not look
  identical on every person's skin.

  This session connected directly to the fairness goals of DermaBridge. Our model
  should not only perform well overall; it should also be able to support people
  with different skin tones. Learning the medical terminology helped me better
  understand the labels in our dataset and reminded me that every row of data
  represents information that may eventually affect a real person's health.

  During the working session, we divided into pairs to combine the datasets and
  train the classification model. Working with a partner made it easier to compare
  our code, discuss errors, and make sure we understood each part of the process.
  It also showed me how collaboration can make difficult technical work more
  manageable.

blockers: |
  My biggest challenge today was dealing with several errors while trying to merge
  the datasets. Some columns and labels were not organized in exactly the same
  way, so the code did not work correctly on my first attempt. I had to check the
  dataset structures carefully and make changes before I could continue.

  I also realized that I needed to return to Project A and revise part of my code
  after I had already started working on Project B. At first, this felt like I was
  moving backward, but I understood that the earlier code needed to be correct
  before I could successfully complete the next stage. This reminded me that each
  part of a research project builds on the work completed before it.

  Another problem occurred when the merged dataset did not save properly to my
  Google Drive. I thought the file had been saved, but I later discovered that the
  file path was incorrect. I had to return to the code, update the path, run the
  saving process again, and confirm that the file appeared in the correct folder.

  Although these errors were frustrating, they taught me to verify my results
  instead of assuming that the code worked simply because it finished running. I
  now understand the importance of checking file locations, reviewing the merged
  dataset, and confirming that the final output contains the expected information.

reflection: |
  Today was a relaxed but productive research day. Even though I experienced
  several problems with my code, I still enjoyed working closely with my group and
  having enough time to focus on the dataset. The errors forced me to slow down,
  examine my work, and understand why each step was necessary.

  We took a few breaks throughout the day, which helped when the coding became
  frustrating. Stepping away from the screen for a short time allowed me to clear
  my mind and return to the problem with more patience. I am learning that taking
  a break does not mean that I am giving up. Sometimes it helps me notice a small
  mistake that I could not see while I was stressed.

  I especially appreciated the session with Dr. Pramanik because it helped connect
  our technical work to the medical purpose of DermaBridge. Learning the meanings
  of the diagnosis labels made the dataset feel less like a collection of numbers
  and more like information connected to real patients and real health concerns.

  I took detailed notes during the session so that I could return to the medical
  terms later, research the ones I did not fully understand, and become more
  familiar with the different lesion categories. Since I am a computer science
  student working on a healthcare-related project, I know that I must continue
  learning both the technical and medical sides of the research.

  By the end of the day, I felt more confident about working with datasets, even
  though I still need more practice. I learned that merging data, correcting
  labels, saving files, and training a model all require patience and attention to
  detail. Today helped me see how the separate parts of our project are beginning
  to come together as we continue building DermaBridge.
